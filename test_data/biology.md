# Biology Notes

## Cell Biology

The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell. It produces ATP through cellular respiration, converting glucose and oxygen into energy, carbon dioxide, and water.

The nucleus contains the cell's DNA and controls gene expression. It is surrounded by the nuclear envelope, which has pores that allow molecules to pass in and out.

The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer that controls what enters and exits the cell. It is selectively permeable, meaning it allows some substances through but not others.

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) comes in two forms: rough ER, which has ribosomes and produces proteins, and smooth ER, which synthesizes lipids and detoxifies chemicals.

The Golgi apparatus processes and packages proteins made by the rough ER and ships them to their destinations inside or outside the cell.

Lysosomes contain digestive enzymes that break down waste materials and cellular debris. They are sometimes called the cell's "cleanup crew."

## Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce oxygen and glucose. It occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells.

The equation for photosynthesis is: 6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

There are two stages: the light-dependent reactions (in the thylakoid membrane) and the Calvin cycle (in the stroma).

In the light-dependent reactions, sunlight splits water molecules and releases oxygen as a byproduct. ATP and NADPH are produced to power the Calvin cycle.

The Calvin cycle uses ATP and NADPH to fix carbon dioxide into glucose. This process does not require light directly and is sometimes called the dark reactions.

Chlorophyll is the pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy. It absorbs red and blue light most effectively and reflects green light, which is why plants appear green.

## DNA and Genetics

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) carries genetic information. It is made of four bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), and cytosine (C). A pairs with T, and G pairs with C.

A gene is a segment of DNA that codes for a specific protein. Genes are passed from parents to offspring through reproduction.

Mutations are changes in the DNA sequence. They can be caused by errors in replication, radiation, or chemical exposure. Some mutations are harmful, some are neutral, and some are beneficial.

DNA replication occurs before cell division. The double helix unwinds and each strand serves as a template for a new complementary strand. The enzyme DNA polymerase carries out this process.

RNA (ribonucleic acid) carries instructions from DNA to the ribosome. Messenger RNA (mRNA) carries the genetic code, transfer RNA (tRNA) brings amino acids, and ribosomal RNA (rRNA) forms part of the ribosome.

Protein synthesis occurs in two steps: transcription (DNA → mRNA in the nucleus) and translation (mRNA → protein at the ribosome).

Dominant alleles mask the expression of recessive alleles. An organism with two identical alleles for a trait is homozygous; with two different alleles it is heterozygous.

Mendel's Law of Segregation states that each organism carries two alleles for each trait and they separate during gamete formation so each gamete carries only one allele.

## Evolution

Natural selection is the process by which organisms with favorable traits survive and reproduce more successfully than those without. Over time, this leads to evolution.

Charles Darwin proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection in his 1859 book "On the Origin of Species."

Adaptation is a trait that improves an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its environment.

Speciation occurs when populations of the same species become isolated and evolve separately until they can no longer interbreed. Geographic isolation is a common cause.

Fossil records provide evidence for evolution by showing how species have changed over time. Comparative anatomy also shows evolutionary relationships through homologous structures.

Genetic drift is a random change in allele frequency in a small population. It can cause traits to become more or less common regardless of survival advantage.

## Ecology

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with their physical environment. It includes both biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components.

A food chain shows the transfer of energy from producers (plants) to primary consumers (herbivores) to secondary consumers (carnivores). Only about 10% of energy is transferred between each level.

Biomes are large geographic areas with similar climates and ecosystems. Major biomes include tropical rainforests, deserts, tundra, grasslands, and temperate forests.

The nitrogen cycle describes how nitrogen moves through ecosystems. Bacteria fix atmospheric nitrogen into a form plants can use. Decomposers return nitrogen to the soil when organisms die.

Symbiosis describes close relationships between species: mutualism (both benefit), commensalism (one benefits, other unaffected), and parasitism (one benefits, other harmed).
