# World History Notes

## World War II

World War II lasted from 1939 to 1945. It involved most of the world's nations and was the deadliest conflict in human history, with an estimated 70-85 million casualties.

The war began when Nazi Germany, led by Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland on September 1, 1939. Britain and France declared war on Germany in response.

The Allied powers included the United States, the United Kingdom, the Soviet Union, and France. The Axis powers included Germany, Italy, and Japan.

The war in Europe ended on May 8, 1945 (V-E Day) with the defeat of Nazi Germany. The war in the Pacific ended on September 2, 1945 (V-J Day) after the US dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.

The Holocaust was the systematic genocide of six million Jews and millions of others by the Nazi regime. It was carried out through concentration camps, mass shootings, and gas chambers.

The Battle of Stalingrad (1942-1943) was a turning point on the Eastern Front. The Soviet victory stopped the German advance and marked the beginning of a German retreat.

The D-Day invasion on June 6, 1944, saw Allied forces land on the beaches of Normandy, France. It was the largest seaborne invasion in history and opened a crucial second front against Germany.

## The Cold War

The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the United States and the Soviet Union from 1947 to 1991. It was called "cold" because there was no direct large-scale fighting between the two powers.

Key events include the Berlin Blockade (1948), the Korean War (1950-1953), the Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), and the Vietnam War (1955-1975).

The Cold War ended with the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991.

The Space Race was a competition between the US and USSR to achieve dominance in space exploration. The USSR launched Sputnik in 1957, the first artificial satellite. The US landed on the Moon in 1969 with Apollo 11.

NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization) was formed in 1949 as a military alliance among Western nations. The Warsaw Pact was the Soviet-led equivalent formed in 1955.

The Berlin Wall was built in 1961 to prevent East Germans from fleeing to West Berlin. Its fall in 1989 symbolized the collapse of Soviet power in Eastern Europe.

The policy of containment, developed by diplomat George Kennan, guided US Cold War strategy. It aimed to prevent the spread of communism beyond its existing borders.

## The Renaissance

The Renaissance was a cultural and intellectual movement that began in Italy in the 14th century and spread across Europe through the 17th century.

It marked a renewed interest in classical Greek and Roman culture, art, and learning. Key figures include Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael, and Galileo Galilei.

The invention of the printing press by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440 helped spread Renaissance ideas across Europe.

Humanism was the intellectual movement at the core of the Renaissance. It emphasized human potential, reason, and achievement rather than purely religious themes.

The Medici family of Florence were powerful patrons who funded many Renaissance artists and thinkers. Their support helped make Florence the center of the Renaissance.

Renaissance art featured realistic depictions of the human body, perspective, and secular subjects alongside religious ones. Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa and Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling are iconic examples.

Nicolaus Copernicus proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system in 1543, challenging the Church's view that Earth was the center of the universe.

## The Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the late 18th century and transformed economies from agrarian and handcraft-based to manufacturing and industry-based.

Key inventions include the steam engine (James Watt), the spinning jenny (James Hargreaves), and the locomotive. These innovations dramatically increased production and changed how people lived and worked.

Urbanization accelerated as people moved from rural areas to cities to work in factories. This led to overcrowded cities, poor working conditions, and the rise of the working class.

Child labor was widespread during the Industrial Revolution. Children as young as five worked in mines and factories in dangerous conditions for very low wages.

The railroads revolutionized transportation, making it faster and cheaper to move goods and people across long distances. By 1850, Britain had over 6,000 miles of railway track.

The Industrial Revolution spread from Britain to Europe and North America through the 19th century. It transformed global trade, increased living standards over time, and set the stage for modern capitalism.

## Ancient Civilizations

Mesopotamia, located between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, is considered the cradle of civilization. The Sumerians developed one of the earliest writing systems called cuneiform around 3200 BCE.

Ancient Egypt developed along the Nile River. The pharaohs were considered gods and ruled for over 3,000 years. The pyramids were built as royal tombs; the Great Pyramid of Giza was built around 2560 BCE.

Ancient Greece developed democracy, philosophy, and foundational works in science and literature. Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle shaped Western philosophical thought. Athens held the first Olympic Games in 776 BCE.

The Roman Empire at its height controlled most of Europe, North Africa, and parts of the Middle East. It spread Latin language, Roman law, and engineering across the known world. It fell in 476 CE.

The Silk Road was a network of trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean. It facilitated the exchange of goods, ideas, religions, and diseases between East and West for centuries.
