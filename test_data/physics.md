# Physics Notes

## Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted on by an external force. This is also called the law of inertia.

Newton's Second Law: Force equals mass times acceleration (F = ma). The greater the mass of an object, the more force is needed to accelerate it.

Newton's Third Law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. If you push against a wall, the wall pushes back with the same force.

Weight is the force of gravity on an object: W = mg, where g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth's surface. Mass is the amount of matter in an object and does not change with location.

Friction is a force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Static friction prevents an object from moving; kinetic friction acts on a moving object.

Momentum is the product of mass and velocity (p = mv). The law of conservation of momentum states that the total momentum of a closed system remains constant.

## Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. It is calculated as KE = ½mv², where m is mass and v is velocity.

Potential energy is stored energy. Gravitational potential energy is calculated as PE = mgh, where m is mass, g is gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s²), and h is height.

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.

Work is done when a force moves an object over a distance: W = Fd. It is measured in joules (J).

Power is the rate at which work is done: P = W/t. It is measured in watts (W). One watt equals one joule per second.

Mechanical advantage allows machines to multiply force. Simple machines include levers, pulleys, inclined planes, wedges, screws, and wheels and axles.

Thermal energy is the total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance. Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles. Heat flows from hot to cold objects.

## Waves

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy through matter or space. Waves have amplitude, wavelength, and frequency.

Frequency is the number of waves that pass a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive wave crests.

The speed of light in a vacuum is approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s. Light is an electromagnetic wave and does not require a medium to travel.

Transverse waves oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel (e.g., light, water waves). Longitudinal waves oscillate parallel to the direction of travel (e.g., sound).

Sound is a mechanical wave that requires a medium. It travels faster through solids than liquids, and faster through liquids than gases. The speed of sound in air is approximately 343 m/s.

The Doppler effect is the change in frequency of a wave as the source moves relative to the observer. A siren sounds higher pitched as it approaches and lower as it moves away.

Reflection occurs when a wave bounces off a surface. Refraction is the bending of a wave as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed.

## Electricity

Ohm's Law states that voltage equals current times resistance: V = IR.

Current is the flow of electric charge, measured in amperes (A). Voltage is the electric potential difference, measured in volts (V). Resistance opposes the flow of current, measured in ohms (Ω).

In a series circuit, current is the same throughout. In a parallel circuit, voltage is the same across each branch.

Electric power is calculated as P = IV (power = current × voltage). It is measured in watts.

Conductors allow electrons to flow freely (e.g., copper, silver). Insulators resist electron flow (e.g., rubber, glass). Semiconductors (e.g., silicon) have conductivity between the two.

Magnetic fields are produced by moving electric charges. Electromagnets are created by running current through a coiled wire. The strength increases with more turns or more current.

## Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed — it is conserved. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that entropy (disorder) in a closed system always increases. Heat naturally flows from hot to cold, never the reverse.

Specific heat capacity is the amount of heat needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1°C. Water has a very high specific heat (4,186 J/kg°C), making it effective at storing thermal energy.
